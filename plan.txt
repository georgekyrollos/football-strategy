Football Strategy Full-Game Solver: Project Explanation
1. What we are doing

The goal of this project is to construct a full-game equilibrium solver for Avalon Hill’s board game Football Strategy, with the aim of producing a publishable extension of Sean McCulloch’s paper, A Game-Theoretic Intelligent Agent for the Board Game Football Strategy.

McCulloch’s paper makes the key observation that each scrimmage play in Football Strategy is naturally a two-player zero-sum matrix game:

the offense secretly chooses a play;
the defense secretly chooses a defense card;
the intersection of those choices determines the result.

He solves that local matrix game using mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium. However, to make the agent care about things like first downs, touchdowns, turnovers, and fourth-down situations, he modifies the raw yardage values with hand-tuned heuristic bonuses and penalties.

Our project replaces that entire heuristic layer with a principled full-game solution.

The idea is:

Instead of assigning a manually chosen utility to each offense-defense chart result, assign it the exact equilibrium continuation value of the full game state it creates.

That means:

a first down is valuable because it leads to a stronger future state;
a turnover is bad because it creates a strong state for the opponent;
a field goal, punt, or fourth-down attempt is chosen based on actual future win value;
clock management, score differential, field position, and down-distance all enter naturally;
no reward shaping or ad hoc constants are needed.

Mathematically, we solve Football Strategy as a:

finite-horizon, two-player, zero-sum stochastic game
	​


and compute equilibrium play for the entire game by backward induction.

2. Rules of the game that we will model

We will initially solve the game using the Pro Style Offense Plays Chart, which is the chart used in your existing code and the most natural point of comparison to McCulloch. The rules allow players to choose among offense charts before the game, but that chart-selection meta-game is not the main object of this paper.

2.1 Pre-game setup

Each player selects which offense-play chart they will use for the full game. Once selected, that chart remains fixed.

A die roll determines the “coin toss.” The winner chooses whether to:

kick off to begin the game, or
receive to begin the game.

If the toss winner chooses to kick off to start the game, the opponent chooses whether to kick or receive to begin the second half.

Thus, the solver needs to track:

h∈{+1,−1}

indicating which team will receive the second-half kickoff. This matters in first-half states, especially near halftime.

We may either:

solve conditional on a chosen opening possession and second-half receiver, or
model the toss decision tree explicitly as a small pre-game strategic layer.

The core game solver works either way.

2.3 Routine of play from scrimmage

After a kickoff, the receiving team becomes the offense.

Each scrimmage play proceeds as follows:

The defense chooses one of ten defense cards:

A,B,C,D,E,F,G,H,I,J

and places it face down.

The offense calls one offensive play from its offense chart.
The defense card is revealed.
The offense play and defense card are cross-indexed on the chart.
The result is applied:
yardage;
time;
down;
first-down marker;
possession;
score, if applicable.

This is exactly a simultaneous-move strategic interaction. The defense chooses without knowing the offense play, and the offense chooses without seeing the defense card.

2.4 Downs and first downs

The offense has four downs to gain at least ten yards.

If it gains the necessary distance, it earns a new first down.
If it fails on fourth down, the defense gains possession at the resulting ball location.
On fourth down, the offense may instead punt.
If within field-goal range, the offense may attempt a field goal on any down.

The solver must therefore track:

ball position;
current down;
yards to first down.

2.7 Punts

Punting is legal only on fourth down.

In the ordinary scrimmage routine, the defense still chooses a card A–J, and the offense chooses Punt. The punt column of the chart determines the result.

Thus, mathematically, punt is a genuine offensive action row inside the offense-defense matrix, not a defense-independent special move.

The instructions also specify:

if the punt carries through the end zone, the return is ignored and the receiving team takes over at its own 20;
if the punt lands in the end zone, the receiving team may choose:
to take the listed runback, or
to down the ball and take possession at its own 20.

This choice is itself resolved strategically by whichever team receives the punt.

2.10 Safeties

4.7 Backward solve order

Without timeout complications, the solve order is:

solve Q4 terminal boundary;
solve Q4 states from τ=0 upward to τ=60;
solve Q3;
solve Q2, whose end boundary maps through halftime into Q3;
solve Q1.

Because τ is “time remaining,” solving from small τ to large τ is backward induction.

With timeouts

Timeouts introduce one wrinkle:

a timeout can cause a play to advance zero clock ticks;
therefore a state may transition to another state with the same τ, but with one fewer timeout.

This is still acyclic, because timeout counts decrease.

So the solve order becomes something like:

quarter descending 4,3,2,1;
time remaining ascending;
total available timeouts ascending.

That guarantees that when evaluating a timeout decision, the successor state with fewer timeouts is already solved.

5. The project in one paragraph

This project solves Football Strategy as an exact finite-horizon zero-sum stochastic game. McCulloch previously showed that each offense-defense interaction can be modeled as a local matrix game, but relied on hand-tuned bonuses and penalties to make the agent value long-run strategic events. We replace those heuristic utilities with recursively computed full-game continuation values. Every chart outcome is evaluated by the equilibrium value of the exact game state it produces, accounting for down, distance, field position, clock, score differential δ, halftime possession, special teams, chance tables, and eventually timeout strategy. The game is solved by backward induction from terminal win/loss states, with a small zero-sum matrix game solved at every reachable scrimmage state. Computationally, the solver combines an exact rules engine, reachability pruning, transition caching, and efficient local LP solves to produce an equilibrium strategy map for the entire board game.
